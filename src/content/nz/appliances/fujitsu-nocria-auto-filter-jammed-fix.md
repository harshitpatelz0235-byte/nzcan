## The Robotic Vacuum on Your Wall

When Fujitsu introduced the Nocria series to the New Zealand market, it completely revolutionized premium home heating. Instead of relying on homeowners to remember to pull out their dirty, dust-caked filters every month and wash them in the sink, the Nocria featured an internal robotic "Auto-Cleaning" mechanism. 

It is an incredibly clever system. When you turn the machine off, a tiny electric motor engages a series of plastic cogs. These cogs physically grab the flexible mesh air filter and drag it across a stiff internal brush. The brush scrapes the dust off the mesh and deposits it neatly into two small plastic "Dust Collection Boxes" located on either side of the chassis. 

The machine literally vacuums itself. 

However, introducing moving robotic parts into a machine that operates in a harsh, dusty environment invariably leads to mechanical complications. After three or four years of faithful service, many Nocria owners across NZ and Australia experience a sudden, terrifying failure: 

When the machine turns off, instead of a gentle hum, you are greeted with a violent, loud *CLICK-CLICK-GRIND-CLICK* noise. The filters become jammed halfway out of the machine, the red error lights begin flashing, and the heat pump refuses to turn back on. 

This comprehensive 2000-word DIY guide explains exactly how the Nocria auto-cleaning system fails, why relying entirely on the robot is a mistake, and how to unjam the gears without cracking the fragile plastic chassis or paying a massive technician callout fee. 

## Understanding the Jam: Why Did the Robot Choke?

The auto-clean mechanism is a masterpiece of micro-engineering, but it relies on several factors operating perfectly in sequence. When the system jams, it is almost never a failure of the electrical motor. It is a failure of basic physical space. 

### Culprit 1: The Overflowing Dust Box (The Most Common Fault)
The Nocria robotic brush is highly efficient at scraping dust. However, that dust is deposited into two very small plastic collection boxes. These boxes have a finite volume. 

The marketing brochure claims the auto-clean system means "maintenance-free operation for up to two years." In the real world of an average New Zealand home with a golden retriever, a cat, and three kids, those boxes fill up completely in about five months. 

Once the boxes are 100% full, the robot brushes keep working, but the dust has nowhere to go. The dust aggressively backs up into the plastic drive gears. The compacted dust turns into solid cement, physically locking the gear teeth together. The motor tries to turn the gears, struggles, and eventually the plastic teeth skip over each other, creating that horrific grinding noise. 

### Culprit 2: The Sticky Filter Mesh
Heat pumps in the lounge or kitchen environment suck in microscopic airborne cooking oils (especially if you deep fry or pan-sear frequently). Over several years, this invisible oil coats the flexible plastic mesh of the air filter. 

The mesh becomes incredibly sticky. When the tiny robotic motor tries to drag the sticky, rigid filter through the tight plastic tracks, the resistance is too high. The filter buckles, jams in the track, and the motor overloads and triggers a system halt. 

### Culprit 3: The Snapped Drive Gear
If a Nocria owner hears the gears grinding due to a full dust box, but ignores the noise for six months, the constant violent skipping of the plastic teeth eventually causes the plastic drive cog to snap in half. 
Once the physical drive gear is broken, the robot is dead. The motor spins freely, but the filter never moves.

## The DIY Fix: Complete Un-Jamming Procedure

If your Nocria is currently jammed, half-open, or flashing red, you must carefully reset the physical environment. Do not force the filters with your hands, as you will permanently strip the plastic gears. 

### Step 1: The Total Power Isolation
The Nocria auto-clean robot operates even when the heat pump appears "off" via the remote. To safely work on the mechanism without it suddenly engaging and crushing your fingers, you must remove all power. 
Go outside to the compressor unit and switch the heavy-duty wall isolator to the **OFF** position. 

### Step 2: Accessing the Dust Boxes
1. Gently lift the massive front exterior cover of the indoor unit until it clicks and locks into the open "up" position. 
2. Look at the extreme left and extreme right sides of the internal chassis. You will see two small, rectangular plastic boxes (often clearly labeled "Dust Box" in the manual). 
3. There is usually a small plastic release tab beneath them. Press the tab and gently slide the boxes forward and out of the machine. 

### Step 3: The Deep Clean
1. Take the dust boxes to your outside rubbish bin. Open them and dump the compacted, felt-like brick of dust. Wash the empty boxes in warm soapy water and dry them completely. 
2. Inspect the flexible mesh filters. If they are jammed halfway, do not yank them. Carefully trace the edges to see where they are caught on the track. Usually, once the dust box is removed, the tension releases, and you can gently slide the filter out. 
3. Wash the mesh filters in the sink with a mild degreasing dish soap to remove years of sticky cooking oil residue. Let them air dry out of direct sunlight. 
4. Take a small, dry toothbrush and aggressively scrub the tiny plastic gear wheels located on the tracks where the filters ride. Dislodge every single speck of compacted dust from the gear teeth. 

### Step 4: Reassembly and The Homing Reset
1. Once the mesh filters are bone dry, carefully slot them back into their designated tracks. Make sure they are sitting perfectly flush and are not buckled. 
2. Slide the clean, empty dust collection boxes back into the sides of the chassis until they firmly click into place. 
3. Close the main front cover of the heat pump. 

**The Critical Homing Sequence:**
Go outside and turn the external power isolator back to **ON**. 

Do not press anything on the remote for five minutes. Because the chassis lost complete power, the internal Nocria computer will "wake up" and immediately realize it does not know where the filters are currently located. 

It will automatically initiate a "Homing Sequence." You will hear the robot engage. The motor will very slowly drag the filters up and down the track exactly once. It does this to count the gear rotations and establish 'Zero' point coordinates. 

Once it finds the start position, it will park the filters, beep once, and go to sleep. Your heat pump is now fully unjammed and ready for winter. 

## When to Call Fujitsu Service (The Fatal Gear Break)

If you performed the painstaking deep clean, scrubbed the gears, and turned the power back on, but during the "Homing Sequence," you hear an agonizing, continuous clicking noise and the filters do not physically move—you have significant mechanical damage. 

You have likely stripped the primary drive gear or snapped the tiny plastic linkage arm attaching the motor to the sweeping track. 

This cannot be bodged or glued. The entire internal auto-cleaning track assembly (often sold by Fujitsu as a completed modular part) must be ordered and replaced. Because this requires dismantling the fragile outer shell of the indoor unit and exposing the high-voltage interior boards to access the robot chassis, this repair strictly falls under the domain of a licensed HVAC technician. 

Expect a repair bill of $300 to $450 NZD for parts and specialized labor. 

## Preventative Maintenance: Don't Defer to the Robot

The Fujitsu Nocria is an exceptional piece of engineering that delivers incredibly high COP (Coefficient of Performance) heat output. But you must not treat the automated system as an excuse to completely ignore the machine. 

To ensure the robot never jams again:
1. **The Quarterly Empty:** Set a reminder on your phone for every three months (e.g., the first day of every new season). Pop the hood, pull out the dust boxes, and empty them. It takes 45 seconds and will save you a $400 repair bill. 
2. **The Annual Wash:** Once a year, preferably in late autumn before the heavy winter heating season begins, manually slide the actual mesh filters out and wash them with dish soap to remove the sticky cooking oil that strains the motor. 
3. **Ignore the Timer:** Do not wait for the flashing "Filter" light on the indoor unit to tell you what to do. The light simply flashes every 400 hours. In a heavily used lounge, the boxes will be over-capacity and jamming the gears long before that 400-hour light triggers. 

## Conclusion: A Clean Track is a Quiet Track
A grinding, jammed Fujitsu Nocria is alarming, but it is rarely a catastrophic electrical failure. The robotic sweeping system is simply screaming out because it is drowning in its own collected dust. By performing a zero-cost DIY intervention—isolating the power, emptying the dust boxes, vigorously cleaning the gear teeth with a toothbrush, and allowing the machine to reset its electronic homing coordinates—you can restore the silent, automated brilliance that made you purchase a Nocria in the first place.
