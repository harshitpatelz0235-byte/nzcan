## The Laundry Room Flood

Simpson washing machines are iconic in New Zealand and Australia. Usually branded as the "Ezi Set" series, they are the definition of a rugged, no-nonsense top loader that lasts for a decade. 

However, because they are so reliable, owners ignore them until something mechanically ages out. 

If you walked into your laundry room today to find a puddle of soapy water spreading across the linoleum floor, do not panic. Before you jump online to buy a replacement machine, you must identify exactly *where* the water is coming from. 

Here are the three most common leak points on a Simpson top loader, ranging from a 5-minute DIY fix to a terminal mechanical failure.

## Cause 1: The External Hose Failure (The Easy Fix)

In 90% of service calls for "leaking washing machines," the appliance itself isn't broken at all. 

Before tipping a heavy, water-filled machine around, you must inspect the external plumbing. Over years of high-speed spinning, the washing machine constantly vibrates against the back wall, physically damaging the hoses. 

1.  **The Inlet Hoses:** Look at the hot and cold hoses connected to the top rear of the machine. The internal rubber O-rings degrade after 5 years, especially on the hot water line. If the leak is a constant, slow drip down the back of the white metal casing, unscrew the hose, replace the 50-cent rubber washer from Mitre 10, and screw it back on tight. 
2.  **The Corrugated Drain Hose:** The grey plastic hose that pumps water into your laundry sink is incredibly brittle. If the machine was pushed too hard against the wall, this hose can easily crack or split. Run a dry paper towel along the entire length of the grey hose while the machine is draining. If the paper towel gets wet, buy a universal replacement hose for $20.

## Cause 2: The Drain Pump Seal (The Intermediate Fix)

If the external hoses are bone dry, but water rapidly gushes out from underneath the machine roughly 20 minutes into the wash (exactly when the machine tries to empty the dirty water), your **Drain Pump** has failed. 

The drain pump is a small electric motor bolted to the bottom of the internal tub. Over the years, bobby pins, coins, and bra wires slip past the wash drum and get sucked into the pump. While they might not completely jam the motor, they tear the delicate rubber seal separating the water from the electrical coils. 

1.  Unplug the machine from the wall. 
2.  Have a second person carefully tilt the washing machine backward at a 45-degree angle. 
3.  Look up underneath the machine. You will see a small plastic pump with two thick black rubber hoses attached to it. 
4.  If the pump casing is visibly dripping water, or if it is covered in crusty white/grey water stains (calcium deposits from old leaks), the pump is dead. 
5.  **The Repair:** You can buy a genuine Simpson replacement drain pump online in New Zealand for roughly $60 to $90. It is held in place by three screws and two wire clips, making it a relatively simple DIY swap using basic pliers. 

## Cause 3: The Central Tub Seal (The Terminal Failure)

If the washing machine only leaks during the violent, high-speed spin cycle, and the water pooling on your floor has a muddy, brownish-red, or rusty tint, your Simpson has experienced a catastrophic failure of the **Central Tub Seal**.

The plastic wash tub you put your clothes into sits inside a larger outer tub that holds the water. At the very bottom center is a massive steel driveshaft connected to the gearbox and motor. 

To stop the 60 liters of wash water from pouring directly down that steel shaft into the motor, there is a heavy-duty rubber tub seal. 

When this seal finally tears after 8 to 12 years of heavy use, water pours down the spinning metal shaft, instantly rusting the internal steel bearings. The "rusty water" on your floor is literally the dissolved metal of your bearings. 

### Is it worth fixing?
**No.** Replacing a central tub seal on a Simpson top loader requires an appliance technician to completely gut the machine. They must remove the agitator, extract the inner tub (which is often welded on by calcification), drop the motor and gearbox out the bottom, and press new bearings into the housing. 

The labor bill alone will exceed $350 NZD. Given that a brand-new, modern Electrolux or Simpson top loader with a warranty costs around $650, a blown tub seal means it is officially time to retire your trusty Ezi Set.
