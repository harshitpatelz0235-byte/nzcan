## The Silent Shut Down

Mitsubishi Electric dominates the New Zealand heating market. Their high-wall "EcoCore" series can be found in homes from Auckland to Invercargill, praised for their whisper-quiet operation and aggressive heating output during winter. 

However, because they are incredibly complex pieces of refrigeration equipment, they rely on a network of highly sensitive internal sensors. 

If something goes wrong—low refrigerant pressure, a stalled fan motor, or a power surge—the heat pump will instantly shut down its compressor to prevent catastrophic mechanical failure. 

To tell you it is broken, it uses the only method it has: the LED lights on the bottom right corner of the indoor unit. Specifically, the **Green Timer Light** will begin a relentless, rhythmic flashing. 

Here is how to decode the flashing and reset the system. 

## Step 1: Count the Flashes (The Error Code)

Unlike modern ovens with digital screens, a standard high-wall Mitsubishi heat pump uses "Morse Code" to tell you what part has died. 

Stand in front of the unit and watch the blinking green timer light. It will flash rapidly a specific number of times, pause for a few seconds, and repeat the sequence. 

Count the flashes. 

*   **2 Flashes:** Indoor coil thermistor error. (The sensor tracking the extreme cold/heat of the indoor copper pipes has short-circuited). 
*   **3 Flashes:** Indoor fan motor error. (The spinning plastic barrel fan inside your living room is jammed or the motor has burned out). 
*   **5 Flashes:** Room temperature thermistor error. (The sensor that tells the heat pump it is 18°C in your living room has failed). 
*   **6 Flashes:** Outdoor unit communication failure. (The brain inside the house cannot talk to the massive compressor outside. Usually caused by a chewed wire or a blown fuse on the outdoor circuit board). 

### The "Defrost" Exception (Main Light Flashing)
**Crucial Distinction:** If the *Timer* light is off, but the main *Operational/Power* light is slowly flashing green, **your heat pump is not broken.** 

During winter, ice builds up on the outdoor spinning fan unit. The heat pump will automatically pause your indoor heating for 5 minutes, reverse its cycle, and melt the outdoor ice. During this "Defrost Cycle," the indoor light slowly blinks. Do not turn the machine off. Wait 5 minutes, and the hot air will return.

## Step 2: The Physical Checks

Before calling a technician, you must rule out the two most common "user error" triggers that cause a blinking green light. 

**1. The Louver Snap:**
Did you recently clean the heat pump? Check the horizontal plastic flap (the louver) that directs the air up and down. On the extreme left and right sides, it clips into tiny white plastic motors. If you accidentally unclipped the louver while wiping it with a cloth, the motor will register a "Stall Error" and flash the green light. Snap the louvers firmly back into their plastic sockets. 

**2. The Suffocated Filter:**
Open the massive plastic front cover of the indoor unit. Pull out the two curved nylon dust nets. Are they completely caked in grey fuzz? Run them under a hot tap, dry them with a towel, and slide them back in. Ensure the massive front plastic cover clicks tightly shut on both the left and right sides. An open cover will trigger a safety kill-switch.

## Step 3: The 5-Minute Master Reset

Heat pumps are essentially computers. If a localized power surge hits your house, the microprocessor inside the Mitsubishi unit can crash, throwing a false flashing green timer light. 

You cannot reset this with the remote control. You must drain the electricity from the motherboard. 

1.  Put your remote control down. 
2.  Walk outside to the massive metal compressor unit. Beside it, bolted to your house wall, there should be a white, weatherproof **Isolator Switch**. Flick it down to "OFF". 
3.  *(Alternatively, go to your home's main switchboard and flip the "Heat Pump" circuit breaker to OFF).*
4.  Leave the electricity entirely off for exactly **5 minutes**. 
5.  Turn the power back on. 
6.  Wait 60 seconds for the internal computers to boot up. 
7.  Press the ON button on your remote control. 

If the unit hums to life, opens the louvers, and starts blowing glorious hot air, congratulations—you have successfully cleared a micro-surge glitch. 

If the louvers open, pause, and the green timer light immediately begins flashing its rhythmic code again, the specified hardware component has genuinely failed. You must call a licensed HVAC technician and tell them exactly how many times the light is flashing to expedite the repair.
