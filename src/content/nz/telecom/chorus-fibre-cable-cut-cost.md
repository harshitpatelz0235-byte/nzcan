# Accidentally Cut Your Chorus Fibre Cable? Here is Who Pays in 2026

<div class='instant-fix-box'>
  <p>If you or your builder have severed the Chorus fibre cable, <strong>stop digging immediately</strong> and call your ISP to report a "Total Loss of Service due to physical damage." Do not try to tape it back together. If the cut happened on your property without using the <em>beforeUdig</em> service, expect a repair bill. Simple indoor fixes cost around $160. Complex underground driveway repairs can cost thousands.</p>

## The "Oops" Moment That Stops Your Internet

It usually happens on a sunny Saturday afternoon. You are doing some landscaping in the front garden, putting in a new fence post, or aggressively weeding near the boundary line. You drive the spade into the dirt, hear a faint *crunch* of plastic, and immediately hear someone inside the house yell, "Did the Wi-Fi just go down!?"

You look in the dirt and see a severed black tube with a few wisps of Kevlar and a tiny, shattered glass strand poking out.

You have just cut your Chorus lead-in cable.

Panic sets in. Fibre optics sound expensive. You imagine a bill for ten thousand dollars arriving in the mail. Before you try to secretly bury the cable and pretend it was a spontaneous network outage, you need to understand exactly how Chorus handles these repairs in 2026, and how the billing actually works.

## Breaking Down the Liability: Who Pays?

Chorus is a wholesale network operator. They own the physical glass cables in the ground, but you pay a retail ISP (Spark, One NZ, Contact) for the internet.

When a cable breaks, Chorus does not just blindly issue massive invoices. They operate on a strict set of geographical and procedural rules to govern liability. Your financial responsibility depends on exactly *where* and *how* the cable was cut.

### Scenario 1: The Public Berm (You Pay Nothing)

If you are digging a new garden on the grass berm outside your physical property boundary (the land owned by the local council), or if a council contractor is digging a trench down the street and hits the cable feeding your house, **you pay absolutely nothing.**

Chorus covers all repairs that happen on public land up to the edge of your legal property boundary. This is deemed standard network maintenance.

### Scenario 2: The "beforeUdig" Protection (You Pay Nothing)

New Zealand has a brilliant, entirely free service called **beforeUdig.co.nz**.

If you are planning to build a retaining wall, put in a pool, or dig a deep trench on your property, you are legally required to submit a request to beforeUdig. They will email you the official architectural plans showing exactly where the power, water, and Chorus fibre cables are buried under your lawn.

*   **The Golden Rule:** If you used beforeUdig, and you meticulously followed their map, but the Chorus cable was actually buried 3 meters away from where the map explicitly stated it was, **Chorus pays for the repair.** By using the service, you shift the liability away from yourself, provided you proved the map was inaccurate.

### Scenario 3: The Blind Dig (You Pay Everything)

This is the scenario that catches 90% of homeowners out.

You decided to plant a lemon tree. You didn't check beforeUdig because it was "just a small hole." You hit the fibre cable buried 300mm below the topsoil.

Because the damage occurred *inside* your property boundary, and you did not perform the legally protected checks, you are 100% liable for the repair costs. Chorus will fix it quickly, but the invoice will be passed through your ISP and added to your next monthly broadband bill.

## How Much Does the Repair Actually Cost?

If you fall into Scenario 3 and have to pay, the cost scales dramatically based on the complexity of the physical damage. It is rarely a flat fee. It is based on time, materials, and heavy machinery.

These are the three most common repair tiers as of 2026:

### Tier 1: The Indoor "Truck Roll" ($150 - $200)
**The Incident:** Your dog chewed the thin yellow patch cable inside the lounge. You accidentally crushed the green fibre plug with a heavy bookshelf. You pulled the wall box off the plasterboard while vacuuming.
**The Fix:** The technician arrives in a van with a pre-made cable or a small fusion splicer. They spend 30 minutes inside your house replacing the plug.
**The Cost:** This triggers a standard "Fault Not Found in Network (User Damage)" truck roll fee. Expect $160 on your next bill.

### Tier 2: The Simple Garden Splice ($300 - $600)
**The Incident:** You cut the black lead-in cable with a spade in the soft dirt of your front garden. The conduit is broken, but both ends of the cable are easily accessible and close together.
**The Fix:** The technician digs a slightly larger hole safely around the cut. Instead of replacing the whole cable back to the street, they use a weatherproof underground splice enclosure. They melt the two broken glass strands perfectly back together, seal it in a black plastic torpedo-tube to keep the rain out, and rebury it. Total time: 1 to 2 hours.
**The Cost:** You are billed for the call-out fee, the expensive waterproof splicing enclosure, and the hourly labor rate. Usually around $450.

### Tier 3: The Civil Nightmare ($1,500 to $5,000+)
**The Incident:** Your driveway contractor brought in a 3-ton excavator to rip up your old concrete. The excavator bucket caught the green Chorus micro-duct (the protective piping) and violently ripped 10 meters of fibre cable entirely out of the ground, shattering the glass core all the way back to the street pillar.
**The Fix:** This cannot be spliced mid-air. The entire underground run from your house to the street cabinet is destroyed. Chorus must dispatch a heavy "Civil Team." They have to lay new underground conduit, potentially drill under a reinstated driveway, and blow completely new fibre strand from the street cabinet all the way to your lounge. Total time: A full day or more.
**The Cost:** You are paying for a multi-man civil engineering crew, traffic management (road cones) if needed, and entirely new infrastructure. These bills frequently exceed $2,500.

*Note: If a hired contractor did this damage, do not pay the ISP bill yourself. Forward the bill immediately to the contractor's public liability insurance provider.*

## "Can I Just Tape It Back Together?"

**Absolutely, unequivocally, NO.**

This is not a 12-volt speaker wire or a garden hose. You cannot strip the plastic back, twist the inner glass together, wrap it in duct tape, and expect the internet to work.

Fibre optic broadband works by firing a microscopic, invisible infrared laser down a glass tube that is only 9 microns wide (about the size of a red blood cell). 

If the glass is snapped, the light instantly shoots out the side into the dirt. Even if you hold the two broken pieces perfectly together with your fingers, the microscopic jagged edges will scatter the laser light, resulting in a 100% loss of signal (the Red LOS light on your modem).

The only way to join two pieces of fibre is to use a heavily calibrated machine called a **Fusion Splicer**. This machine uses an electrical arc to melt the glass at precisely 1,700°C, fusing the two strands into one continuous, optically perfect core. Taping it will only achieve one thing: getting dirt on the glass, which will force the technician to cut even more cable off to find a clean section to splice.

## The Immediate Steps After You Cut the Cable

If you have just cut the cable, follow this protocol to minimize the financial and technical damage.

1.  **Stop Digging:** Put the shovel down. Do not dig around the cable to "see how bad it is." You might pull the cable tighter and shatter the glass further down the line, turning a $300 Tier 2 fix into a $2,000 Tier 3 fix.
2.  **Cover It Up (Temporarily):** Throw a plastic bucket or a piece of tarp over the exposed, broken cable ends. If it starts raining, muddy water will seep into the micro-duct tubing. If water travels down the tube towards the street cabinet, the technician will have to use compressed air to blow the water out before they can fix it, adding hours to your labor bill.
3.  **Do Not Call Chorus:** Chorus will not talk to you directly. They only accept fault tickets from retail ISPs.
4.  **Call Your ISP Immediately:** Call Spark, Skinny, 2degrees, or whoever bills you every month. Use this specific phrasing to ensure they escalate the ticket correctly:
    *   *"I need to report a complete outage due to physical third-party damage. The Chorus lead-in cable on my property has been physically severed by a digger/spade. The ONT LOS light is solid red. I need to authorize a Chorus technician dispatch for a physical cable repair."*
5.  **Acknowledge the Fee Prompt:** The phone agent is legally required to read you a disclaimer stating, *"You may be charged a fee if the fault is deemed to be within your property boundary and caused by user damage."* Say "Yes, I accept." If you argue with them, they cannot dispatch the technician, and you will stay offline.

## How to Prevent It Happening Again

The rules for burying fibre cables in New Zealand have changed repeatedly over the last decade.

In the early days of the UFB (Ultra-Fast Broadband) rollout, some cowboy contractors used "shallow trenching," burying the fibre just a few inches beneath the lawn to save time. 

Today, strict regulations demand fibre be buried much deeper (often 300mm to 450mm) and covered with a bright orange or green warning tape slightly above the cable. However, you can never guarantee how deep an older installation actually is.

**The Golden Rule:** Never, ever break ground with machinery, or dig a hole deeper than 100mm with a hand tool, without first looking at the plans from **beforeUdig.co.nz**. It takes 5 minutes, it is free, and it is the only bulletproof way to protect your wallet from a $5,000 Chorus invoice.
