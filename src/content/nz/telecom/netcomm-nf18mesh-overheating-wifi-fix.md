# NetComm NF18MESH Dropping Wi-Fi? Check the Temperature

<div class='instant-fix-box'>
  <p>To fix a NetComm <strong>NF18MESH</strong> that is dropping Wi-Fi connections, you must resolve its thermal airflow. Do not hide the router inside a closed wooden TV cabinet. Ensure the <strong>clear plastic factory shipping film</strong> is completely peeled off the top and bottom vents. Finally, you must use the included plastic "foot" bracket to stand the router <strong>strictly vertically</strong>. Lying it flat blocks the bottom air intakes and causes the CPU to overheat.</p>

## The "Silent Reboot" Problem

The NetComm NF18MESH is one of the most widely deployed modems in New Zealand. ISPs like Slingshot, 2degrees, and Orcon shipped hundreds of thousands of these white boxes to customers. 

When correctly configured, the NF18MESH is a highly capable Wi-Fi 5 router. 

However, it has one major design flaw: it runs incredibly hot. 

Clients frequently call tech support to report that their internet "keeps pausing." Netflix buffers every 30 minutes, or their phones get kicked off the Wi-Fi entirely and refuse to reconnect for roughly three minutes. 

You look at the router during these dropouts, and the lights are changing from solid green, turning off entirely, flashing red, and then slowly turning green again.

**Your router is not broken. It is panicking.**

The router is experiencing "Thermal Throttling." The internal computer processor has exceeded its safe operating temperature (usually around 85°C / 185°F). To prevent the silicon chip from literally melting and catching fire, the router's safety code kicks in and forces it to reboot completely to cool down.

Here is exactly how to stop this from happening.

## Fix 1: The Factory Plastic Trap

When you receive a brand new NF18MESH in the original cardboard box, the shiny white plastic casing is tightly wrapped in a thin, almost invisible layer of protective cellophane shipping film.

Incredibly, this clear film wraps directly over the top and bottom air vents. 

A massive percentage of New Zealanders pull the router out of the box, plug the cables in, and put it on the shelf **without taking the plastic wrap off.**

Because the router contains zero mechanical cooling fans, it relies entirely on passive airflow to cool the electronics. If the plastic wrap is covering the vents, the router is essentially wearing a winter coat in a sauna. It will begin thermal-shutting down within 48 hours.

Look closely at your router. Use your fingernail on the edge of the case. Peel off any remaining protective films.

## Fix 2: The Vertical "Chimney" Rule

As humans, we universally dislike looking at ugly internet routers. Our natural instinct is to hide the flashing lights out of sight. 

You take the NF18MESH, push the antennas down, and lay the router completely flat on its side underneath the television so it blends in with the Sky decoder box. 

**This is a fatal networking sin.**

The internal circuit board of the NF18MESH is engineered around the "Chimney Effect." 
*   **Bottom Vents:** Cold air is naturally pulled into the vents on the bottom panel.
*   **The Heatsink:** The cold air runs over the metal heatsink on top of the CPU, absorbing the heat.
*   **Top Vents:** Because heat naturally rises, the hot air escapes out the heavy venting on the top panel.

When you lay the router flat on its side, the bottom intake vents are pushed flat against the wooden TV cabinet, choking them completely. The heat has nowhere to go. It pools inside the tight plastic casing, cooking the electronic components, and destroying the delicate copper traces connecting the Wi-Fi antennas.

1.  Find the small, U-shaped plastic foot bracket that came in the original box.
2.  Slide the foot onto the bottom of the router.
3.  Stand the router completely **upright** (vertically). 
4.  Point the two antennas straight up at the ceiling like rabbit ears.

## Fix 3: The "Closed Cupboard" Oven

Even if the router is standing perfectly upright with the plastic wrap removed, it needs fresh, room-temperature air to breathe. 

If you place the NF18MESH inside a modern entertainment unit, shut the wooden door to hide the cables, and run the router 24/7 next to a hot PlayStation 5 or an AV amplifier, you have created a convection oven.

The router sucks in the hot air exhausted by the PlayStation, gets hotter, exhausts even hotter air, and then sucks that same incredibly hot air back in again. 

Routers must be placed "Out, Up, and Open." 
*   **Out:** They must be in the open air of a room.
*   **Up:** They should be placed waist-height or higher on a bookshelf (the floor is cold, but the signal is blocked by furniture; the top of a shelf gives the best Wi-Fi range and best airflow).
*   **Open:** Never place them inside cabinets, cupboards, or behind thick hardcover books.

## When to Ask for a Replacement

If you have perfectly positioned the NF18MESH upright on a high bookshelf in the open lounge, and it is *still* too hot to physically touch after an hour of use, the internal thermal paste has dried out and failed. 

Consumer electronics do have a finite lifespan. Just like the brake pads on your car, a router that processes 500GB of Netflix data a month running 24/7 will eventually degrade.

Do not try to disassemble the router and fix the thermal paste yourself. It is a leased piece of ISP hardware. 

1.  Unplug the power cord immediately.
2.  Call your ISP (or log into their online chat).
3.  Say: *"My provided NF18MESH router is extremely hot to the touch and constantly rebooting itself multiple times a day. It is well-ventilated, but I believe it is experiencing hardware failure and could be a fire risk."*
4.  ISPs are terrified of liability regarding "fire risks." They will not argue with you or make you run complicated ping tests. They will immediately flag the unit as end-of-life and courier you a fresh replacement router for free.
