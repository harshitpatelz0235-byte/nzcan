# Chorus ONT Beeping Every 15 Mins? How to Silence It Forever

<div class='instant-fix-box'>
  <p>The beeping sound coming from your Chorus ONT every 15 minutes is a low-battery alarm. The backup battery inside (or beside) the ONT has died. To stop it immediately, open the battery compartment, unplug the white connector from the old battery, and the noise stops. Replace it with a 12V 7Ah sealed lead-acid battery from Bunnings or Jaycar NZ for around $15 to $25 NZD.</p>

## Why Your Chorus ONT is Beeping

If you have been lying in bed at 2am listening to an electronic beep coming from that white box on the wall near your front door, you are not going crazy and your internet is not broken. That beep is coming from the **Chorus Optical Network Terminal (ONT)**, and it is telling you that its internal backup battery is flat.

Let me explain what this battery actually does and why Chorus put it there in the first place.

When New Zealand switched from copper phone lines to fibre optic broadband, we gained speed but lost something important. Old copper phone lines carried their own power. During a power outage, your landline phone still worked because the telephone exchange sent electricity down the copper wire. This was a safety feature. If your house lost power during a storm, you could still call 111 for help.

Fibre optic cables do not carry electricity. They only carry light. So when your power goes out, your Chorus ONT loses power, and your home phone connected to the ONT goes dead. To solve this safety issue, Chorus installed a backup battery in every ONT. This battery was designed to keep the voice port alive for about 8 to 12 hours during a power cut.

The problem is that these batteries have a limited lifespan. In the NZ climate, a sealed lead-acid battery typically lasts 3 to 5 years. If your Chorus fibre was installed in 2021 or 2022, the battery is now reaching the end of its life in 2026. When the battery can no longer hold a charge, the ONT detects this and starts beeping to alert you. Most models beep once every 15 minutes. Some beep every 10 minutes. A few annoying models beep every 5 minutes.

The internet itself is completely unaffected. Your Netflix will keep streaming. Your gaming will keep working. The beep is only about the phone backup power.

## How to Identify Your ONT Model and Battery Location

Before you can fix this, you need to figure out which ONT model you have. Look at the white or grey box mounted on the wall where the fibre cable enters your house. It is usually near the front door, in the hallway, or in the garage.

### Nokia ONT Models (Most Common in NZ)

The **Nokia G-240W-A** is the most widely deployed ONT in New Zealand. It is a rectangular white unit about the size of a small book. On this model, the battery is located in a **separate external battery box** mounted on the wall beside or below the ONT. The external box is connected to the ONT via a short cable with a white plug.

### Huawei ONT Models

The **Huawei HG8245H** and **HG8240H** are the second most common. On these units, the battery is typically **inside the ONT itself**, accessible by removing the bottom panel or a small door on the underside.

### Calix ONT Models

Some rural NZ areas use **Calix** ONTs. These are less common and usually have the battery in an external enclosure similar to the Nokia setup.

## Step-by-Step: Silencing the Beep by Removing the Battery

This is a 5 minute job that requires no tools on most models. I am going to cover both the external battery box and internal battery scenarios.

### Method A: External Battery Box (Nokia and Similar)

1.  **Locate the External Box:** Look at the wall near your ONT. There should be a smaller white rectangular unit. It may have a small status LED on the front.
2.  **Open the Lid:** Most external battery boxes have a flip-up lid or a panel that slides off. There are no screws on the standard NZ Chorus models. Just press the tab and lift.
3.  **Find the Battery:** Inside, you will see a black rectangular battery about the size of a large smartphone. It is usually a **12V 7Ah** or **12V 9Ah** sealed lead-acid (SLA) battery.
4.  **Disconnect the White Plug:** There is a white plastic connector attached to the top of the battery. Pull this connector straight out. It should come free with a gentle tug. **The beeping will stop immediately.**
5.  **Remove the Old Battery:** Lift the old battery out of the enclosure. It will feel heavy for its size because it is full of lead and acid (safely sealed, not leaking).

### Method B: Internal Battery (Huawei and Similar)

1.  **Turn the ONT Over or Look Underneath:** You may need to look at the underside of the unit. There is usually a small panel held in place by a single screw or a sliding latch.
2.  **Open the Panel:** Use a Phillips-head screwdriver if there is a screw. Otherwise, slide the panel off.
3.  **Disconnect and Remove:** Same as above. Pull out the white connector, then lift the battery out. Beeping stops.

### Important Safety Note

These batteries are sealed and safe to handle with bare hands. However, do not puncture them, crush them, or throw them in your regular rubbish bin. They contain lead and sulfuric acid and must be disposed of at a battery recycling point. Most **Bunnings** and **Mitre 10** stores have free battery recycling bins near the entrance.

## Buying the Replacement Battery in NZ

If you still have a landline phone plugged into your ONT and you want the backup power feature to work during power outages, you should replace the battery rather than just removing it.

### Where to Buy

| **Bunnings Warehouse** | 12V 7Ah SLA | $18.90 | Look in the electrical section near the security alarm batteries |
| **Jaycar NZ** | SB2486 (12V 7Ah) | $22.50 | Staff know exactly what you need if you say 'Chorus ONT battery' |
| **Mitre 10** | 12V 7Ah Gel Battery | $19.95 | Not all stores stock this, call ahead |
| **PB Tech** | UPS Replacement 12V 7Ah | $24.99 | Also works, same specifications |

The key specifications you need are **12V** voltage and **7Ah** (amp-hours) capacity. The battery must be **SLA (Sealed Lead Acid)** or **AGM (Absorbed Glass Mat)** type. Do not use a lithium battery or a car battery. These have different voltage characteristics and could damage the ONT charge controller.

### How to Install the New Battery

1.  Place the new battery into the battery compartment. Match the orientation of the old battery. The terminals should face the same direction.
2.  Connect the white plug back into the battery terminals. You will feel it click into place.
3.  Close the compartment lid or panel.
4.  The ONT may show an amber or orange **Battery** LED for a few hours while it charges the new battery. This is normal. After 6 to 12 hours, the LED should turn green or turn off entirely.

## When You Do NOT Need to Replace the Battery

Here is an honest assessment that a paid technician will never tell you. If you do not use a landline home phone (and most NZ households do not in 2026), you do not need this battery at all. The battery exists purely for voice service backup during power outages.

**You can safely leave the battery disconnected if:**

*   You do not have a landline phone plugged into the ONT
*   You rely on your mobile phone for emergency calls
*   You have a UPS (Uninterruptible Power Supply) on your router for internet backup

**You should replace the battery if:**

*   You have an elderly family member who relies on the landline
*   Your area has frequent power cuts and poor mobile coverage
*   You have a monitored medical alarm that uses the landline port

## Why the Beeping Gets Worse in Winter

NZ users consistently report that the beeping starts or gets more frequent during the colder months (June to August). This is not a coincidence. Lead-acid batteries are temperature-sensitive. In cold conditions (below 10 degrees Celsius), the battery capacity drops significantly. A battery that was barely holding on during summer will cross the 'dead' threshold in winter, triggering the alarm.

If you live in a colder region like Queenstown, Wanaka, Dunedin, or the Mackenzie Country, your ONT battery will die faster than one in Auckland or Northland. Plan on replacing it every 3 years in cold climates versus every 4 to 5 years in warmer parts of NZ.

## What to Say to Chorus (If You Want Them to Do It)

If you are not comfortable doing this yourself, you can ask Chorus to replace the battery. However, be aware that they may charge a **$160 truck roll fee** for sending a technician. That is a lot of money for a 5-minute job with a $20 battery.

If you want to try getting it done for free, call your ISP (Spark, Slingshot, 2degrees, etc.) and say:

> 'My Chorus ONT is producing a continuous low-battery alarm. I believe the backup battery has reached end of life. Under the Chorus property access agreement, the ONT is Chorus-owned infrastructure and battery maintenance should be covered. Can you please raise a fault ticket with Chorus for a battery replacement?'

Some ISPs will raise the ticket as a 'fault' rather than a 'maintenance request,' which can sometimes result in a free technician visit. Your success with this approach depends on your ISP and the particular Chorus field team in your area.

## Preventing Future Battery Issues

A couple of practical tips to extend the life of your next ONT battery:

1.  **Ventilation:** Make sure the battery compartment is not sealed inside a closed cupboard with no airflow. Heat is the number one killer of lead-acid batteries. If your ONT is mounted inside a hallway closet, leave the closet door slightly open.
2.  **Power Stability:** If your house has flickering lights or voltage fluctuations (common in older NZ homes with original wiring), consider putting a surge protector on the ONT power supply. Voltage spikes can damage the battery charge controller inside the ONT.
3.  **Annual Check:** Once a year, open the battery compartment and look for any signs of swelling or white powder on the battery terminals. Swelling means the battery is failing. White powder (lead sulfate crystals) means the terminals have corrosion and need a wipe with a dry cloth.
