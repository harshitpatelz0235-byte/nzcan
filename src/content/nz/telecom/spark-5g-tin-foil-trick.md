# Spark 5G Wireless Modem: The "Tin Foil Trick" for Better Signal

<div class='instant-fix-box'>
  <p>If your Spark 5G Wireless Broadband speed is inconsistent or dropping to 4G, you can boost the signal using the "tin foil trick". Create a curved piece of cardboard covered in aluminium foil and stand it behind the modem (facing the nearest Spark cell tower). This acts as a parabolic reflector, concentrating scattered 5G radio waves into the modem's internal antennas, frequently delivering a 30% to 50% speed increase.</p>

## Why Spark 5G Wireless Broadband Sometimes Sucks

In New Zealand, 5G Wireless Broadband is marketed as the ultimate alternative to fibre. No digging up the driveway, no waiting for Chorus installers, just plug the modem into a wall socket and you have Gigabit internet. That is the marketing promise.

The reality, as many Spark customers in Auckland, Wellington, and Christchurch have discovered, is highly dependent on physics.

5G technology uses higher frequency radio waves than 4G. In the NZ spectrum allocations, Spark primarily uses the 3500MHz (3.5GHz) band for its fast 5G service. High-frequency waves carry massive amounts of data, which is why 5G is so fast. But they have a critical weakness: **they have terrible penetration**.

A 5G signal struggles to get through concrete walls, thick timber framing, and especially the modern double-glazed windows with "Low-E" (low emissivity) metallic coatings common in new Kiwi builds. While the cell tower down the street might be pumping out 800Mbps, your Spark modem sitting on the kitchen counter might only be receiving 50Mbps of that signal, and randomly dropping back to 4G when it rains.

Before you invest $500 in a professional external antenna installation, you need to understand how the Nokia FastMile modem (the tall cylindrical unit Spark uses) actually receives its signal, and how a roll of kitchen foil can manipulate those radio waves in your favour.

## The Physics of the "Tin Foil Trick"

This is not a conspiracy theory or an old wives' tale. The "tin foil trick" works on the exact same physical principles as a satellite dish or the giant radio telescopes at Warkworth.

Your Spark Nokia 5G modem has omnidirectional antennas inside it. This means it tries to listen for signals in a 360-degree circle. But the 5G signal from the Spark tower is only coming from one specific direction.

When you place a curved sheet of aluminium foil behind the modem, you are creating a **Parabolic Reflector**.

Here is what happens:
1.  The 5G radio waves from the tower hit your house. Some pass through the wall and hit the modem directly.
2.  However, many waves miss the modem by a few inches on either side.
3.  These "missed" waves hit the curved tin foil behind the modem.
4.  Because aluminium is highly reflective to radio frequencies, the waves bounce off the foil.
5.  If the foil is curved correctly (a parabola), it focuses all those scattered waves directly onto the modem in the center, dramatically increasing the signal density exactly where the internal antennas need it.

## Step-by-Step: How to Build Your Reflector

You do not need an engineering degree for this. You need a piece of cardboard, some kitchen foil, and tape.

### Step 1: Gather Materials
*   A piece of stiff cardboard, about 40cm wide and 30cm high. A flattened cereal box or courier box works perfectly.
*   Standard aluminium kitchen foil (heavy duty is better if you have it, as it tears less easily).
*   Sellotape, masking tape, or a glue stick.

### Step 2: Cover the Cardboard
Cover one entire side of the cardboard with the tin foil. Make it as smooth as possible. Wrinkles scatter the radio waves instead of focusing them, so smooth the foil down flat. Tape it securely on the back so it does not peel off. The shiny side or dull side does not matter for radio frequencies; both reflect equally well.

### Step 3: Create the Curve (The Parabola)
Tape a piece of string or a smaller strip of cardboard horizontally across the back (the non-foil side) to bend the main piece of cardboard into a gentle curve. It should look like a half-pipe or a very shallow satellite dish. The curve is vital—a completely flat piece of foil will just bounce the signal straight back instead of focusing it inward.

### Step 4: Determine the Tower Direction
This is the most critical part. You must know where the Spark 5G tower is.
1. Download the **Network Signal Info** or **CellMapper** app on your phone, or use the website cellmapper.net on your computer.
2. Select New Zealand and Spark as the provider, and filter for 5G/NR.
3. Locate your house on the map and find the nearest green dot (tower).
4. Note the compass direction of the tower relative to your house.

### Step 5: Positioning the Modem and Reflector
Place your Spark 5G modem in a window or against an external wall that faces the direction of the tower.

Now, place your curved tin foil reflector **BEHIND** the modem, so the foil curve wraps around the back of the modem. The open side of the curve must point exactly toward the wall facing the Spark tower.

The distance between the modem and the foil matters. Start with the foil about 5cm behind the modem. Run a speed test on your phone or laptop. Move the foil back an inch. Run another test. You are trying to find the "focal point" of your homemade dish. When you see the highest download speed and the lowest ping, stop moving it.

## Diagnosing Your 5G Signal Strength

The Spark modem has an LED ring on the top that indicates signal strength, but it is not very accurate. To see if the tin foil trick is actually working, you need to log into the modem's advanced interface and look at the raw numbers.

1.  Open your browser and type **192.168.1.254** or **192.168.8.1** (check the base of your modem for the exact IP).
2.  Log in using the default admin password printed on the sticker.
3.  Navigate to **Status** > **Mobile Network** or **5G Status**.

You are looking for two specific metrics:

### RSRP (Reference Signal Received Power)
This measures the raw strength of the signal. It is measured in negative decibels (dBm). Closer to 0 is better.
*   **Poor:** -110dBm to -120dBm
*   **Fair:** -95dBm to -109dBm
*   **Good:** -80dBm to -94dBm
*   **Excellent:** -60dBm to -79dBm

### SINR (Signal to Interference & Noise Ratio)
This measures the quality of the signal (how loud the signal is compared to background noise). Measured in positive decibels. Higher is better.
*   **Poor:** 0dB to 5dB
*   **Fair:** 6dB to 12dB
*   **Good:** 13dB to 20dB
*   **Excellent:** 20dB+

When you set up your tin foil reflector, watch these numbers. If your RSRP improves from -105dBm to -90dBm, and your SINR jumps from 5dB to 15dB, your download speeds will likely double.

## The Negative Side Effect: Wi-Fi Rejection

There is a major catch to the tin foil trick that you need to factor into your home network design. Aluminum foil reflects *all* radio waves, including the Wi-Fi signal your modem transmits to your devices.

If your modem is sitting on your TV cabinet in the lounge, and you put a sheet of foil behind it facing the window, the foil will block the Wi-Fi from reaching the back half of your house. The bedrooms behind the foil will become dead zones.

This is why the tin foil trick is best used when:
1.  The modem is located at the extreme edge of the house, against an outside wall.
2.  Or, you have turned off the Spark modem's Wi-Fi entirely and are using a separate Mesh Wi-Fi system (like TP-Link Deco or Google Nest) connected via Ethernet to distribute the internet throughout the house.

## When Foil Is Not Enough: The External Antenna Upgrade

The tin foil trick is a brilliant, zero-cost diagnostic tool. If it significantly improves your speeds, it proves that your local Spark tower has capacity, but the signal penetration into your house is the bottleneck.

If the cardboard aesthetic is not working for your living room decor, or if you need even more stability, you have proven that an external antenna will solve your problem permanently.

In 2026, most new residential 5G connections in NZ that experience poor indoor signal require a **MIMO directional antenna**. This is a white panel mounted on your roof or eaves, pointing directly at the cell tower. A cable runs from the antenna, through the wall, down to a new 5G receiver inside.

### How to Get Spark to Pay for It

External antennas and their installation can cost $400 to $600. However, if you are a Spark Wireless Broadband customer experiencing speeds below the promised tier (usually below 50Mbps on a 5G plan), Spark will often cover the cost of the antenna and professional installation to retain you as a customer.

Call Spark Mobile Support (not standard broadband support, use **0800 800 163**) and use this specific script:

> "I am on the Spark 5G Wireless Broadband plan. My indoor signal metrics are poor, with an RSRP of [your number, e.g., -110dBm] and low SINR. My speeds fluctuate badly during peak hours. However, I have tested the connection with a temporary directional reflector and confirmed the speeds improve dramatically when signal gain is increased. Because my house construction (e.g., Low-E glass/brick) is blocking the 3.5GHz signal, I require an external MIMO antenna installation to receive the service I am paying for. Can you please raise a service request for a technician assessment?"

Spark retains ownership of the external antenna, but they will send a contracted technician (usually Downer or UCG) to mount the panel on your roof and drill the cable run into your house for free under their fair use and service guarantee policies.

## The "Modern Windows" Problem in New Zealand

If you live in a new subdivision built after 2020 (like Hobsonville Point in Auckland or Rolleston in Christchurch), your house is your own worst enemy for 5G.

Modern NZ building codes mandate significant thermal efficiency. The primary way builders achieve this is through "Low-E" (low emissivity) double glazing. The glass is coated with an invisible, microscopic layer of metal oxide (often silver, zinc, or titanium).

This metallic layer is brilliant at reflecting heat back into your living room during winter. Unfortunately, a solid sheet of metal oxide is also absolutely brilliantly effective at blocking radio frequencies. A Low-E window acts as an almost perfect shield against the 3.5GHz 5G signal.

If you have these windows, placing your Spark modem on the windowsill is actually the *worst* place for it. The signal will penetrate a standard timber-and-plasterboard wall much better than it will penetrate a metallic-coated window. Move the modem behind a solid wall instead, and re-test your speeds.
