# Contact Energy Broadband: Why You MUST Return Their Modem (Or Else)

<div class='instant-fix-box'>
  <p>If you cancel Contact Energy broadband, <strong>you do not own the modem.</strong> You must return the modem and its power supply within 30 days of disconnection. Contact will mail a pre-paid return courier bag to your address. Package the modem and plug, and drop it at an NZ Post shop. If you fail to do this, a non-return fee (usually $100 to $150) will be automatically added to your final utility bill.</p>
</div>

## The "Free" Modem Trap

Bundling your electricity and broadband is incredibly popular in New Zealand. Contact Energy is a major player in this space in 2026, offering attractive discounts if you let them handle both your power and your internet.

When you sign up, a shiny new Wi-Fi modem arrives in the courier. The promotional material said "Free Modem Included," so you naturally assume it is yours.

**It is not yours.**

This is the biggest point of friction when Kiwis switch ISPs. Read the fine print of your Contact Energy Broadband terms and conditions. The modem is supplied on a *rental* or *loan* basis. You are allowed to use it for free as long as you remain a paying customer. The moment you break that relationship—whether you move house, switch to Electric Kiwi, or cancel the service—Contact Energy wants their hardware back.

If you don't send it back, they will bill you for it. This is called the **Non-Return Fee (NRF)**, and it catches thousands of New Zealanders by surprise every month.

## How the Return Process Actually Works

Contact Energy does not expect you to pay for the return shipping. The process is designed to be automated, but it relies on you taking action. Here is the timeline of what happens when you cancel your broadband:

### Day 1: The Cancellation
You call Contact Energy (or your new ISP initiates the transfer) and your broadband is scheduled for disconnection. The automated system triggers a hardware return request.

### Days 2-7: The Courier Bag Arrives
Within a week, an empty, pre-paid, tracked NZ Post courier bag will arrive in your physical letterbox. It usually contains a brief instruction sheet. If you are moving house, make sure you give Contact Energy a forwarding address or a work address to send the bag to *before* you move out of the property.

### The Packing Rules: What Must Go in the Bag
You need to put the equipment in the bag. But what exactly do they care about? The warehouse technicians receiving these bags log returns based on barcodes.

**You MUST include:**
1.  **The Modem:** This is the main unit. This is the item with the MAC address and serial number that they are actually tracking.
2.  **The Power Supply Unit (PSU):** The black wall plug and cable that powers the modem. Without this, the modem is useless to them and cannot be refurbished.

**You SHOULD include (but probably won't be charged if you lose them):**
1.  **Ethernet Cables:** The yellow, blue, or white cables with plastic clips on the ends.
2.  **DSL Cables / Splitters:** The grey telephone cables used for older VDSL connections.

*Insider Tip:* If your puppy chewed the yellow Ethernet cable, do not panic. Put the main modem and the power plug in the bag and send it. The warehouse teams sorting thousands of returns a week are matching serial numbers, not auditing $2 generic cables. As long as the core hardware is there, the non-return fee will be waived.

### The Drop-Off
Seal the bag. Because it is pre-paid, you do not need to queue at a post office counter unless you want a physical receipt. You can:
1.  Hand it over the counter at any **NZ Post Shop** or **Paper Plus** offering postal services.
2.  Book a free courier pick-up from your house via the NZ Post website (using the tracking number on the bag).
3.  Drop it into a large red NZ Post parcel drop box.

**Crucial Step:** Take a photo of the tracking number on the bag before you drop it off. This is your only proof that you sent it.

## The 30-Day Deadline and the Penalty

From the date your broadband service is officially disconnected, a 30-day countdown timer begins in Contact Energy’s billing system.

If that tracking number is not scanned as "Delivered" at their central warehouse within 30 days, the system automatically intervenes. It generates an invoice for the replacement cost of the hardware and adds it to your account balance.

### How Much Is the Fee?
The fee varies slightly depending on when you signed up and exactly which modem model you were supplied (e.g., an older NetComm NF18ACV vs a newer Wi-Fi 6 mesh unit). In 2026, the standard charge is typically between **$100.00 and $150.00 NZD**.

Because Contact Energy also provides your electricity, they have significant leverage. If you refuse to pay the final bill because you are angry about the modem charge, they will send the entire utility debt to a collection agency like Baycorp. This will put a devastating black mark on your credit rating, making it difficult to get a credit card, a car loan, or even rent a new house in New Zealand.

It is absolutely not worth damaging your credit file over a piece of white plastic networking gear.

## What if the Courier Bag Never Arrives?

This is the most common excuse, and while it is often true (NZ Post loses things, mail gets stolen), **it does not exempt you from the fee**. The burden of responsibility remains entirely on you.

If you have cancelled your service and 7 days have passed without an empty return bag showing up:

**Do not wait.** The 30-day timer is ticking regardless of whether you have the bag or not.

1.  Call **Contact Energy on 0800 224 624**.
2.  Tell the agent: *"I cancelled my broadband service last week, but the pre-paid hardware return bag has not arrived at my address. Can you please dispatch a replacement bag today to [Your Address] and put a note on my account extending the return deadline?"*

By forcing them to put a note on your account, you create a paper trail proving that their logistics failed, not your memory. If you are wrongly charged the fee on day 31, the customer service team can see the note and instantly reverse the charge.

Alternatively, if you are impatient, you can ask the agent if you can courier it back at your own expense. They will provide you with the correct warehouse address. Send it tracked (it will cost you about $8.50 via NZ Post) and email them the tracking number. This is often faster and less stressful than waiting for a replacement bag.

## "But the modem is junk! I want to throw it away."

Even if the modem provided terrible Wi-Fi speeds, even if the plastic has yellowed in the sun, and even if it randomly dropped your connection while you were with Contact—you still cannot throw it away.

Contact Energy operates a hardware refurbishment program to minimize e-waste. When your old router arrives at the warehouse, it is wiped, factory reset, sanitized, and reboxed to send to the next customer. If it is genuinely broken, it is sent to an authorized e-waste recycling facility where the heavy metals and plastics are processed safely.

If you throw it in your general household rubbish wheelie bin:
1.  You will absolutely be charged the $150 non-return fee.
2.  You are contributing toxic electronic waste (circuit boards, solder, capacitors) straight into a New Zealand landfill.

## I Have Upgraded My Router. What Should I Do With the Contact One?

If you are currently a Contact Energy broadband customer but you followed one of our other guides and bought your own high-end mesh Wi-Fi system (like a TP-Link Deco or Google Nest), you are now using **BYOD (Bring Your Own Device)**.

Your Contact-supplied modem is now sitting entirely useless in the bottom of a wardrobe.

**Should you send it back early?**

No. You must hold onto it until you officially close your broadband account.

There is a very specific technical reason for this. In the event that a digger cuts the fibre optic cable on your street, or rain gets into the Chorus cabinet, you will lose your internet connection.

When you call Contact Energy technical support to report the outage, the Tier 1 support agents can only run remote diagnostic tools on **their own supplied hardware**. If you tell them you are using an ASUS Router, they will refuse to raise a fault ticket with Chorus. They will blame your ASUS router and tell you to call the manufacturer.

To get a Chorus fault ticket raised, you MUST be able to pull the Contact Energy modem out of the wardrobe, plug it into the fibre box, and let the support agent "see" the modem fail on their diagnostic dashboard.

Keep the Contact modem safe in its box at the top of the cupboard. Think of it as an insurance policy for when things break. Only put it in the courier bag on the day you finally change ISPs.
